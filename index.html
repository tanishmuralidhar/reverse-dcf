Methodology

A reverse discounted-cash-flow flips an ordinary DCF on its head. Instead of forecasting cash flows to estimate what a company is worth, it takes today’s price as given and solves for the assumption that justifies it — here, the free-cash-flow growth rate implied over the forecast horizon.

The model is a two-stage DCF on free cash flow (operating cash flow minus capital expenditure). The explicit period grows base FCF at the solved rate for the chosen horizon; a Gordon-growth perpetuity captures everything after. Because free cash flow is already a levered, after-tax cash flow to equity holders, discounting it at the cost of equity yields the intrinsic equity value directly, and dividing by shares outstanding gives value per share. A bisection solver finds the growth rate that sets value per share equal to the current price.

Free cash flow is a poor valuation base for banks, insurers, and other financials — those names are flagged. Figures are sourced from Yahoo Finance and may be delayed or restated. This tool is for educational purposes and is not investment advice.
